Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Where are the group housing units and institutions located in Boston?
    • Rationale: Looking at the available census data, I saw there were various types of group and institutional housing, e.g. juvenile detention centers, nursing homes, and other (including homeless shelters and group homes). Although a grew up in Portland, OR, a city with a major homelessness crisis, I realized that I don't really know where resources like homeless shelters are located, so I wanted to learn more about this aspect of Boston.
  2. How have corporate ownership rates and owner occupancy rates changed over time in Boston?
    • Rationale: I moved to the Boston area from Seattle, a city which has increasingly become a tech hub over the past decade. I, too, was a tech worker, and lived in the Downtown area among the many relatively new offices of Amazon, Apple, and Meta. However, from the articles I read and from personal experience, I know that this process of corporatization can lead to increased cost of living and higher rates of evictions. I wanted to understand how the story of corporate ownership had played out in Boston over the past two decades.
  3. How are corporate ownership rates and owner occupancy related to each other?
    • Rationale: Corporate ownership and owner occupancy are both factors that can impact eviction rates, although in opposite directions. The articles talked about the relationship between each of these factors and eviction rates, but I wanted to learn more about how they related directly to each other.
  4. What does the demographic distribution of Boston look like?
    • Rationale: Both of the articles I read talked about how evictions disproportionately effect majority non-White neighborhoods. I thought that exploring the demographic distribution of the city would provide useful groundwork for future investigations, and also help familiarize me with the lay of the land.

Exploratory Analysis

Datasets:

  • Census and Corp Ownership and Occupancy Over Time. This file contains information about owner occupancy and corporate ownership by year (2004 to 2024) for 21 neighborhoods in Boston (available here), as well as census data "aggregated to approximate Boston neighborhood boundaries" from the year 2020 (available here). The census data contains demographic information like age and race, as well as housing information like number of total housing units and number of vacant units.
  • Boston Neighborhood Zipcodes. This file contains a list of boston neighborhood names and their corresponding zipcodes.
  • Zip Codes. This is a geojson I downloaded from "Analyze Boston" (available here), which maps polygons (representing different neighborhoods) to their corresponding zipcodes and neighborhood names.
  • Boston geojson. This is a geojson I downloaded from GitHub, which maps polygons to neighborhood names (reason for the use of this second geojson is explained in the Visualizations section).

Having looked at corporate ownership rates a few different ways, I went on to investigate owner occupancy rates. First, I created a line plot similar to Figure 8:

These trend lines look a lot more flat than the corporate ownership lines. Some neighborhoods like Brighton droped from ~0.4 to ~0.2, but some, like Allston, barely changed at all. For my second question,

"How are corporate ownership rates and owner occupancy rates related to each other?"

I wanted to explore the relationship between the two fields. I compared the values from 2024 in a scatterplot:

Viewing the data this way (as opposed to two line charts) makes the relationship between these fields much clearer. We actually do see a strong correlation between the two rates: The R-squared value of the trendline is 0.77, with p < 0.0001. We can see that the slope of the trendline is negative, meaning that as owner occupancy rate increases, corporate rate decreases. This makes some intuitive sense, since more local owners means less property for corporate owners and vice versa. According to the Housing Justice Beyond the Emergency page, number of evictions tend to increase with corporate ownership rates, and decrease with owner occupancy rates, so it will be interesting to dig into this relationship in future explorations.

Finally, I moved on to question three:

"What does the demographic distribution of Boston look like?"

As I mentioned earlier, information about race and age had been flattened in the data so, for example, counts of people identifying as Hispanic and 18+ would be one column, while Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders aged 0-17 would be another. Total population also had three columns (0-17, 18+, and all). This made it somewhat hard to visualize demographic data on the same graph. Consequently, I selected all rows containing age and race information, and created a pivot table. I then created two calculated groups (race and age) by splitting the combo names (e.g. "white_all" into "white" and "all"). I then created a hierarchy of age under race, so I could see the different age group counts within each race. Lastly, I filtered out the "total" group and the "all" column per group to get the following visualization:

As we can see from this figure, there were a lot of White adults in Boston in 2020. This was by far the largest bar in the chart. Interestingly, counts of individuals aged 0-17 were more evenly distributed between races, with slightly more Black and Hispanic children than White children. Perhaps this points towards a trend in the shifting demographic composition of the city over time (see Facing History and Ourselves). The linked page notes that in 2020, about 45% of Bostonians identified as White. Since we have the 2020 census data, we can verify that this claim plays out in our data.

Indeed, we see this checks out. While White was the largest single race in 2020, it was actually in the slight minority overall. We see that Black and Hispanic populations are the second largest, and American Indian / Alaska Natives are the smallest.

Summary

We first investigated how corporate ownership and owner occupancy rates have changed over time. We observed that corporate ownership rate has gone up a lot from 2024, with some areas experiencing as high as a 12x increase, with the lowest rate still above 2x (more than 100% increase from 2004 to 2024). Owner Occupancy rate went down a little, particularly for a few neighborhoods like Brighton, but generally stayed steadier. When we visualized the 2024 corporate ownership rate and the 2024 owner occupancy rate together, we saw a strong negative correlation between the two. We then took a deeper dive into the city's demographics, and saw that while White adults constitute the largest single population, Boston's youth are more diverse. We also visualized the distribution of demographics across neighborhoods on our map. Lastly, we combined our two main directions of exploration (corporate ownership / owner occupancy and demographics) by comparing the maps of corporate ownership and owner occupancy rates with the maps of demographic data. Outside of my actual findings, I also took away a few lessons about keeping things simple (my headache with the chain of census data to neighborhood+zipcode to geojson), and the utility of restructuring data for easier analysis.
